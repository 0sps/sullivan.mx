Nanotechnology Project

Sean Sullivanapril 2020

For an entire semester, I worked alongside three engineering students to complete two projects. 

The first was a microfluidics project studying cell adhesion of yeast. Specifically, our task was to determine the effect that a treatment would have in eliminating yeast buildup in a microchannel. My group created and designed a microchip with multiple channels that supported such fermentation, and tested the ability of saline (in varying concentrations) to shear the yeast off the microchannel walls. Due to unforeseen circumstances, the experiment was not able to be fully conducted, however all research and design work can be found in the Microfluidics section of the website.

The second project involved creating a lab-on-a-chip capable of detecting a specific disease using only a drop of blood. The team decided to pursue Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) after considering multiple other diseases such as ovarian cancer and tuberculosis. The lab-on-a-chip designed by the team is capable of taking a drop of blood, filtering it to receive plasma, and then detecting the presence of the p24 protein, a protein specific to HIV. This project culminated in an NIH grant proposal and poster presentation. All information regarding this project can be found in the Nanotechnology section of the website, including the various design stages, presentations, and grant proposal.

Nanotechnology Project
Introduction

The goal of the nanotechnology project was to create a NANOLYSER, a Nanofunctionalized Assay Nested in an Onboard Laboratory Yielding Specific Expeditious Results, to detect a disease with only a small sample of human blood. Our group chose human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) as the disease of choice. Since increasing access to testing for HIV could help protect many more lives from this dangerous disease, using nanotechnology to
create a new innumoassay that works both faster an cheaper than existing
methods could prove incredibly useful.

Design Stages

As part of brainstorming and finding the best way to make a microchip, my team underwent a series of design stages, or opportunities to refine their work.

The first design stage was essentially a brainstorming session. It allowed my team to create multiple ideas and settle on one tentative design to be further iterated and improved in future stages. We originally chose Tuberculosis and ovarian cancer as possible diseases for testing, honing in on specific proteins present in diseased individuals which could be detected in the presence of certain antibodies.
A nanolyser (lab-on-a-chip) design was created for detecting tuberculosis as part of this work. The chip was designed to have two inlets, one for a blood sample and one for a colloidal gold suspension to be injected. The chip was then to combine the two solutions and use an LED-based photo receptor assembly to detect a color change indicative of TB. This color change was thought to be activated by the colloidal gold nanoparticle suspension combined with specific TB antibodies, which created a unique color when reacting. The design was later abandoned.

Design Stage 2:
In the second design stage, my team finalized our disease choices, and determined which methods to use to detect them. The group changed their focus to detecting human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) in human blood. To do this, they would construct a nanolyser with one tank to hold purified blood plasma and gold nanoparticles, and another to hold waste. The chip would filter human blood to separate [most] plasma from red blood cells, and send them to a reservoir prefilled with gold nanoparticles and p24 antibodies– ones specifically designed to react with HIV proteins. Therefore, if HIV were present in the sample, proteins on its surface would react with the solution and create a red solution visible to the naked eye.

Design Stage 3:
In the final design stage provided drawings, specifications, and realistic methods by which to construct the lab-on-a-chip..

The chip was designed to have one inlet port for a human blood sample, driven in by an external peristaltic pump and syringe. The blood would pass through bifurcation channels, designed to filter out most red and white blood cells from the sample. Large cells would continue down the main channel, stopping at a waste resovoir, while the prufiied plasma would be driven into a new tank via a fingerpump. The final tank would be prefilled with gold nanoparticles and p24 antibodies, creating an instant reaction with the sample as it entered. It would turn blue if HIV’s p24 proteins were present, and red if they were not. Clinicians could easily see these color changes as they conduct the test.
The chip was specifically designed for easy cleaning, as it was divided into two halves that could be quickly removed to wash in between tests.

CAD Chip Design

My team'schip design was focused on ease of use, portability, and durability.

The chip consists of two halves; a top and a bottom. The top half contains a fingerpump, while the bottom half features all the main channels, resovoirs, and bifurcation channels.

Nanolyser Top

Not only functioning to cover the channels of the other half, the top contains a hole for easy reagent loading and a finger pump to move the sample from the plasma chamber to the detection chamber.

NANOLYSER Bottom

The bottom contains most of the important features as the chip. Firstly, it includes the fluid inlet and filtration chamber. This chamber consists of five bifurcation channels that filter the plasma out of the blood, changing its appearance from opaque to translucent. The chamber also contains separate tanks-- one for holding red and white blood cells, and one for holding purified plasma.

Finally, the bottom half contains the detection chamber. This chamber matches up with the round hole in the top to allow healthcare providers easy access to reagent loading prior to initiating a test.

Experimental Procedure

As part of the microfluidics project, my team created a comprehensive experimental procedure for testing a microchip.

The procedure aims to test the shearing capability of saline when added to microchannels with bioadhered yeast.

First, it involves incubating yeast for several minutes, first outside and then inside the microchannel. It then requires a low-pressure flush, a gentle and slow movement of water through the microchannel, to remove
any yeast cells that did not bond to the biofilm during the incubation time. Then, a solution of water or one of three concentrations of saline
will be run through the chip for one minute. Afterward, the amount of remaining yeast cells in the microchip was analyzed using a microscope.
This process was repeated at different fluid velocities, so as to test the additional affect velocity may have on yeast cell adhesion.
Through this procedure, we sought to determine whether the salt content of a saline solution could cause more yeast cells to shear and fall out
of the microchannel.

Experimental Statistics

My group worked extensively on experimental statistics knowledge as part of the microfluidics experiment.

Each team member individually completed an assignment tailored to statistics, thereby learning terminology and procedures associated with statistical analysis. My team learned how to set up an experiment by creating null and alternative hypothesis, as well as methods to test them using t-tests. We used this knowledge in the microfluidics experiment, where we determined null and alternative hypotheses, as well as criteria for rejecting their preconceptions. We were required to interpret different scenarios and determine null and alternative hypothesis, as well as interpret results from a t-test and determine whether they constituted a rejection of the null.
